The Engineer's Role — NFPA 1002

  • NFPA 1002 is the standard for Fire Apparatus Driver/Operator Professional Qualifications
  • The driver/operator (DO) is responsible for the safe transport, positioning, and pump operation of the apparatus
  • The DO is on the rig before the call — and stays with it until the rig is back in quarters and ready for the next response
  • Two NFPA 1002 chapters frame the position:
  • Chapter 4 — General Requirements: inspection, maintenance, driving, backing, defensive driving
  • Chapter 5 — Pump Operations: tank, hydrant, drafting, relay, pressure management
  • The DO is judged less on speed and more on consistency — the rig that doesn't crash and the line that doesn't lose pressure

Daily Apparatus Check

  • Conducted at shift change — signed off in the apparatus log
  • Walk-around (clockwise from driver door):
  • Body damage, mirrors, lights (low/high beam, brake, turn, scene, warning)
  • Tires — tread depth, sidewall, lug nut indicators, valve caps, air pressure
  • Compartments — latched, inventoried, equipment secured
  • Engine bay — oil, coolant, power steering, washer fluid, belts, hoses
  • Cab — SCBA seats charged, MDT/radio responsive, emergency lighting cycle test
  • Pump panel — primer cycle, tank-to-pump, gate valves, intake/discharge caps, water level
  • Air system — build to governor, leak-down test, low-air warning
  • Document deficiencies on the OOS log; notify officer immediately if anything is safety-critical
Crew Roles — Pre-Roll
  • Officer: Confirms address, radio assignment, tactical worksheet
  • FF1 (Backstep right): Attack line, irons, primary search
  • FF2 (Backstep left): Hydrant, water supply, backup line
  • DO: Best route, apparatus position, pump readiness
  • All: Seatbelts before wheels move — non-negotiable
Common Pre-Roll Failures
  • Address not confirmed; rig rolls toward wrong cross street
  • SCBA not donned enroute — delays entry on arrival
  • Accountability tags forgotten
  • Cab doors not latched — warning chime ignored
  • Hose load left disturbed from previous call

Driving with Due Regard

  • Due regard — the legal duty to operate an emergency vehicle with the safety of all persons in mind, regardless of right-of-way granted by warning lights
  • Ohio Revised Code references:
  • ORC 4511.03 — Emergency vehicles must come to a complete stop before proceeding through any red signal or stop sign
  • ORC 4511.041 — The driver of an emergency vehicle is not relieved from the duty to operate the vehicle with due regard for the safety of all persons
  • Code 3 — lights and siren; Code 1 — normal traffic flow, no warning devices
  • Intersection rules: stop, scan all four directions, make eye contact with civilian drivers, proceed lane-by-lane
  • Backing — never alone; spotter at left rear, two taps to stop, mirror sweeps every 5 seconds
  • Use the engine retarder/Jake brake on grades; ride the throttle, not the service brakes
  • Weight transfer in turns — reduce speed to 60% of posted limit on curves; full water tank shifts the center of gravity

Communication & Radio Awareness

  • Enroute — monitor dispatch and primary tactical channel; acknowledge updates
  • On arrival — the officer gives size-up; the DO listens for the water-supply call
  • Mark your hydrant on the radio: "Engine 12 has a hydrant at Main and 5th, forward laying 200 feet of 5-inch."
  • Switch to the assigned tactical or fireground channel as directed
  • If command requests a water-supply update, give a number: PSI, GPM, or "tank water remaining"
  • If you hear a MAYDAY, stop non-essential traffic, dedicate a discharge to the RIT line, and report your readiness
Storz Hydrant (5-inch LDH)
  • Sexless quarter-turn coupling — no male/female threads
  • Connect, rotate 1/4 turn clockwise, lock pin engages
  • Faster connection, less likely to cross-thread
  • Standard for forward-lay supply on most modern apparatus
  • Open hydrant slowly to charge supply line; full open after charged
Threaded Hydrant (NST/NH)
  • National Standard Thread — 4.5 inch steamer, two 2.5 inch outlets
  • Use hydrant wrench to remove cap and open valve
  • Hand-tight first — avoid cross-threading; finish with wrench, do not over-torque
  • Common in older systems; carry adapters (Storz-to-NST) for cross-compatibility
  • "Down to charge, up to shut" — nozzle convention to remember rotation direction

Forward Lay vs Reverse Lay

  • Forward lay — engine drops at the hydrant (FF2 jumps off), then drives to the fire laying supply line behind it
  • Engine arrives at scene fully positioned for attack
  • Best when hydrant is between dispatch route and the fire
  • Reverse lay — engine arrives at fire, drops attack lines, then drives back to hydrant laying supply line
  • Useful when the hydrant is past the fire, or when a second engine establishes supply
  • Pump operator at the hydrant; another driver pumps the attack engine
  • Department SOP usually defines the default lay; deviation requires officer approval

Apparatus Placement

  • The first piece sets the geometry of the entire scene. Get it wrong and every truck after has to compensate
  • Position past the structure (about 25 feet) so the front of the rig clears the building — gives the truck company room to set up if a ladder is requested
  • Stay out of the collapse zone — minimum 1.5x building height for unstable structures
  • Check overhead — power lines, tree limbs, awnings; aerial apparatus needs vertical clearance
  • Egress — never block your own retreat; a second engine should be able to pass on at least one side
  • Wheels chocked, brake set, transmission in pump gear (or neutral with parking brake, per manufacturer)
  • Highway incidents — block one lane wider than your work area, wheels turned away from crew, scene lights toward oncoming traffic

Pump Engagement & Pressure Management

  • Sequence (typical midship pump):
  • Truck stopped, parking brake set
  • Transmission to neutral
  • Pump shift switch to PUMP — wait for green "OK to pump" indicator
  • Transmission to DRIVE — rear wheels stay still, pump turns
  • Open tank-to-pump valve; pump now pressurized from booster tank
  • Charge attack line to target pressure (typical 1.75-inch handline at 100 PSI nozzle + friction loss)
  • Transition to hydrant supply: open intake gate slowly, close tank fill, monitor compound gauge for residual pressure
  • Use pressure governor mode (PSI) for handlines; RPM mode only for relay or master stream as SOP allows
  • Friction loss rule of thumb: FL = C × Q² × L (hydraulic calculator card on the panel for the engineer who isn't fast at math under stress)
  • Watch the gauges, not the fire — that's the FF1's job

Generator, Lighting, & Secondary Duties

  • Once water is flowing and pressure is stable, the engineer rotates to secondary duties:
  • Scene lighting — deploy generator, raise the light tower, position cord reels for the interior crew
  • Ladders — throw a ground ladder to a second-floor window for the search team's bailout
  • Tools — stage irons, pike poles, K-12 saw, thermal imager at the front step
  • SCBA bottles — pull spare bottles from the compartment, stage them at rehab
  • Hydration — water bottles to crews coming off air; rehab area set up
  • Salt / shovel — ice control around the rig and hose bed in winter
  • Backup hose lines — pull a second 1.75 to the front door before it's needed
  • Every minute spent setting up secondaries early saves three minutes when command asks for them

Pump Operator as Command's Eyes

  • The DO has the only continuous 360-degree view of the building once interior crews go inside
  • Report changes to command:
  • Smoke conditions changing color, volume, velocity, density (the four attributes of fire behavior)
  • Fire showing in new openings
  • Structural movement — sagging eaves, spalling brick, settling porch
  • Civilian arrival or self-evacuation
  • Approaching apparatus or police, blocked egress, downed power lines
  • Water supply forecasting — if your tank is at 25% and you're not on a hydrant yet, that's a command notification, not a panic
  • RIT-line readiness — one discharge gated and pre-charged for MAYDAY response, ready instantly

Quiz

01 Which NFPA standard governs Fire Apparatus Driver/Operator Professional Qualifications?

A) NFPA 1001
B) NFPA 1041
C) NFPA 1002
D) NFPA 1500
C) NFPA 1002

02 "Due regard" means the emergency vehicle driver must:

A) Always have right of way under lights and siren
B) Operate with the safety of all persons in mind regardless of warning devices
C) Drive at any speed necessary to reach the call
D) Never stop at red lights when responding
B) Operate with the safety of all persons in mind regardless of warning devices

03 A Storz coupling connects to a hydrant by:

A) A quarter turn clockwise — sexless coupling, no threads
B) Five full clockwise turns with a hydrant wrench
C) Threading the male end into the female outlet
D) A friction-fit clamp tightened with a strap wrench
A) A quarter turn clockwise — sexless coupling, no threads

04 In a forward lay, the engine:

A) Drops a firefighter at the hydrant, then drives to the fire laying supply line behind it
B) Arrives at the fire first, then drives back to the hydrant
C) Pumps from tank water only, never connecting to a hydrant
D) Stages two blocks away and waits for command
A) Drops a firefighter at the hydrant, then drives to the fire laying supply line behind it

05 A typical 1.75-inch handline is supplied at what nozzle pressure (smooth-bore not used)?

A) 50 PSI
B) 75 PSI
C) 100 PSI
D) 150 PSI
C) 100 PSI

06 When backing the apparatus, the spotter should be positioned:

A) At the front bumper
B) At the left rear of the apparatus, in the driver's mirror sight line
C) Inside the cab giving verbal directions
D) Wherever convenient — backing alone is acceptable in an empty bay
B) At the left rear of the apparatus, in the driver's mirror sight line

07 The correct sequence for engaging a midship pump is:

A) Drive → Pump shift → Park brake → Tank-to-pump
B) Pump shift → Drive → Park brake → Tank-to-pump
C) Park brake → Neutral → Pump shift → Drive → Tank-to-pump
D) Tank-to-pump → Pump shift → Drive → Park brake
C) Park brake → Neutral → Pump shift → Drive → Tank-to-pump

08 When positioning the engine at a residential structure fire, you should:

A) Stop directly in front of the door for shortest hose stretch
B) Park on the lawn for stability
C) Pull into the driveway and block the truck company
D) Pull approximately 25 feet past the structure to leave room for the truck company
D) Pull approximately 25 feet past the structure to leave room for the truck company

09 Which of the following is NOT a typical secondary duty of the engineer once water is flowing?

A) Throwing ground ladders for crew bailout
B) Conducting interior primary search
C) Staging spare SCBA bottles at rehab
D) Pulling a backup 1.75-inch line to the front door
B) Conducting interior primary search

10 The four attributes of fire behavior the pump operator should report to command are:

A) Heat, light, smoke, sound
B) Color, smell, texture, temperature
C) Volume, velocity, density, color
D) Speed, height, angle, distance
C) Volume, velocity, density, color
