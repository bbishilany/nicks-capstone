The Hard Numbers

  • Sudden cardiac events are the leading cause of on-duty firefighter death — year after year, NFPA reports them as roughly 40–50% of total LODDs
  • The IAFF found 66% of career firefighter line-of-duty deaths from 2002–2019 were attributed to occupational cancer
  • The Firefighter Behavioral Health Alliance has documented hundreds of firefighter and EMS suicides annually — a number widely understood to be under-reported
  • The fire service kills its own people slowly, on a delay, with diseases that are largely preventable or detectable
  • This class is built on the premise that knowing the data and acting on it are different things — and the gap is what we are here to close

Cardiac Risk — The #1 Killer

  • Strenuous fireground activity, alarm-response adrenaline, and dehydration multiply existing cardiovascular risk
  • NFPA's annual report consistently lists overexertion / stress / medical events as the leading nature of fatal injury
  • Modifiable risk factors:
  • Hypertension — check it; treat it
  • Cholesterol — lipid panel at annual physical
  • Body composition — not vanity, it's load on the heart under air
  • Tobacco / nicotine / smokeless — quit programs are covered by most EAPs
  • Sleep apnea — common, under-diagnosed, treatable
  • NFPA 1582 — Standard on Comprehensive Occupational Medical Program for Fire Departments — defines the annual medical evaluation, including cardiac screening protocols
  • Know your numbers: BP, resting HR, lipid panel, A1c. Get them annually. Track them.

Occupational Cancer

  • Firefighters have a higher risk of certain cancers than the general public — mesothelioma, testicular, multiple myeloma, and others have shown elevated rates in studies (NIOSH, IARC)
  • Carcinogens come from combustion byproducts, off-gassing PPE, and contaminated equipment — absorbed through skin, inhaled through soot, ingested at the kitchen table without realizing it
  • The cleanest fireground habits:
  • Mask on at the door; mask off only after gross decon
  • Gross decon at the scene — soap, water, brushes; bag the gear before getting in the cab
  • Wipes for skin (face, neck, hands, jaw line) before getting back on the rig
  • Shower within the hour at the station; clean uniform on; bunker gear stays out of the living areas
  • Hood swap — hoods absorb the most particulate; rotate to a clean one
  • The IAFF Cancer Awareness Initiative is the rallying point — "no shortcuts on decon, ever"

Mental Health & Suicide Prevention

  • Cumulative trauma exposure (PTSI — Post-Traumatic Stress Injury) is the rule, not the exception, over a 25-year career
  • Risk patterns:
  • Sleep disruption, irritability, withdrawal from family
  • Increased alcohol use; "two beers becomes six"
  • Loss of joy in activities the person used to love
  • Giving away possessions, sudden calmness after long depression, talking about being a burden — these are the late warning signs
  • The intervention is not a script — it is a relationship. The line that opens the door:
  • "I've noticed you don't seem like yourself lately. I'm worried. What's going on?"
  • Then listen. Don't fix.
  • Resources: 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (press 1 for veterans), IAFF Center of Excellence (Maryland), department peer support team, EAP
  • Calling for a friend is not snitching — it is the job

Fitness — NFPA 1583

  • NFPA 1583 — Standard on Health-Related Fitness Programs for Fire Department Members — lays out a department-level program: assessment, exercise prescription, equipment, and the role of a Peer Fitness Trainer (PFT)
  • The job demands cardiovascular endurance + strength + mobility — not a single one will get you home from a working fire alone
  • Practical in-station programming:
  • 2–3 strength sessions per shift week (compound lifts, kettlebell work, sled drags)
  • Zone 2 cardio 2–3 times per week (heart-rate based, conversational pace, 30–45 min)
  • One high-intensity day — intervals, sled push, stair climb in gear
  • Mobility/recovery daily — 10 minutes is enough
  • CPAT (Candidate Physical Ability Test) standards are the floor, not the ceiling — if you can barely pass entry standards as a 12-year FF, that is a wake-up call, not a victory
  • Train for the call you have not had yet — the one with the heavy victim drag and the third-floor advance
Department Resources
  • Station gym — equipment maintained, hours protected from chores
  • EAP (Employee Assistance Program) — free, confidential counseling, often 6–8 sessions per issue
  • Peer Support Team — trained members who respond to critical incidents and to-hand-off conversations
  • Chaplain Services — available regardless of religious affiliation
  • Behavioral Health Program — clinically trained, fire-service-fluent providers
  • IAFF Center of Excellence — in-patient treatment specifically for first responders
Personal Accountability
  • Sleep — the recovery shift exists; sleep then, eat right, hydrate
  • Hydration — water bottle on the rig; refill at every call
  • Nutrition on shift — the firehouse meal can be made of vegetables; the dessert tray is not mandatory
  • Annual physical — show up; do the lab work; ask the questions
  • Decon — every time, even on the trash fire
  • Mental check-in — when was the last time you asked someone how they actually were?

Camaraderie & Intervention Culture

  • Brothers and sisters notice first — before family, before doctors, before anyone
  • The kitchen table is where the change happens, not the chief's office
  • The intervention is not heroic — it's "I noticed, I care, here's the number, I'll go with you"
  • Three-second rule for asking the hard question:
  • One: notice something is off
  • Two: decide you will say something today, not Monday
  • Three: open with curiosity, not diagnosis — "How are you, really?"
  • Confidentiality matters; gossip is the opposite of camaraderie
  • If a peer is in active crisis, do not leave them alone — stay, call peer support / 988, hand off in person when help arrives

Schedule Evolution — 24/48 vs 24/72

  • The traditional 24/48 schedule was built around a department staffing model, not around recovery science
  • Sleep researchers and the IAFC have noted that 48 hours is often insufficient recovery for a high-call-volume shift
  • Departments moving to 24/72 typically report:
  • Better self-reported sleep quality
  • Reduced sick time use
  • Improved family/parenting quality of life
  • Operational continuity (more days off allows for second jobs and education without burning out)
  • Mental health (MH) days — departments are increasingly recognizing them as legitimate, separate from sick time, used to protect against the cumulative load
  • The schedule is a department decision, but the personal piece is: do you use your off-shift to recover, or to grind?

The NFFF 16 Life Safety Initiatives

  • Published by the National Fallen Firefighters Foundation after the Tampa 2 Summit (2004)
  • The fire service's own roadmap to reducing line-of-duty deaths and injuries
  • Several initiatives speak directly to today's topics:
  • #1 — Cultural change toward leadership, accountability, and personal responsibility
  • #6 — Develop and implement national medical and physical fitness standards
  • #7 — Create a national research agenda and data collection system
  • #13 — Provide firefighters and their families with access to counseling and psychological support
  • #14 — Public education must receive more resources and emphasis
  • Read all 16 at everyonegoeshome.com — share them with your crew
